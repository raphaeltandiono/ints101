Procedure & Operationalization

Operationalizing Variables (Examples)

Key variables from the hypotheses are operationalized using SNA metrics and ecosystem performance indicators calculated per period (e.g., quarter/year).

H1: Network Fragmentation (Density) & Recovery Speed

Fragmentation measured by ΔDensity (change in density). Recovery Speed measures time for funding to return to trend.

ΔDensity (Crisis) = Density[t_crisis] - Density[t_pre-crisis]

Recovery speed measured via time-series analysis of funding volume.

H2: Bridging Capital & Early-Stage Resilience

Bridging capital measured via Betweenness Centrality. Early-stage resilience via %Δ in deal volume.

EarlyStageResilience = %Δ(Early Stage Deals Volume [Crisis])

Higher average betweenness indicates more bridging capital.

H3: Centralization & Late-Stage Stability

Centralization measured via degree centralization or core density. Late-stage stability via deviation from trend.

LateStageStability = Deviation(Late Stage Funding Volume [Crisis])

Higher centralization indicates dominance by a few VCs.

Analysis Plan

Quantitative Analysis: Regression Modeling

Quantitative analysis will utilize panel data regression models to investigate the relationship between VC syndication network structures and ecosystem resilience. Resilience indicators serve as dependent variables, while network structure metrics and their changes during crisis periods act as independent variables, controlling for other factors. Interaction terms are key to testing how network structure moderates the impact of downturns (H1-H3).

Model Specifications (Illustrative):

Models will be specified to test each hypothesis, often using interaction terms:

  • H1 (Fragmentation & Recovery): RecoverySpeed = β0 + β1(ΔDensitycrisis) + Controls + ε (Predict β1 > 0)
  • H2 (Bridging & Early-Stage): EarlyStageResilience = β0 + β1(Downturn) + β2(BridgingCapitalpre) + β3(Downturn * BridgingCapitalpre) + Controls + ε (Predict β3 > 0)
  • H3 (Centralization & Late-Stage): LateStageStability = β0 + β1(Centralizationpre) + β2(Downturn * Centralizationpre) + Controls + ε (Predict β1 > 0 or β2 > 0)

Subscripts omitted for brevity. 'pre' indicates pre-crisis measure.

Addressing Endogeneity:

Potential endogeneity of network variables will be addressed primarily using lagged independent variables. Instrumental variable (IV) techniques will be explored, preceded by formal endogeneity tests (e.g., Durbin-Wu-Hausman) where appropriate instruments can be theoretically justified and validated.

Endogeneity Test (Conceptual Example for ΔDensity)

Using Durbin-Wu-Hausman test (regression form):

  • Regress endogenous variable (ΔDensity) on instruments (Z) and exogenous variables (X).
  • Obtain residuals (û).
  • Include residuals (û) in the main structural equation (e.g., for RecoverySpeed).
  • Test significance of the coefficient on û. Significance suggests endogeneity.
Mock Test Result: Coeff(û) = 0.15, p = 0.02 --> Endogeneity likely.

Requires finding valid instruments (correlated with ΔDensity, uncorrelated with RecoverySpeed error).

Hypothesis Testing (H1, H2, H3):

Each hypothesis will be tested by examining the sign, magnitude, and statistical significance of the relevant coefficients (β's) in the regression models, particularly the main effects and interaction terms related to network structure and downturn periods, after addressing endogeneity concerns.

Qualitative Analysis: Thematic Analysis & Triangulation

Crucially, qualitative thematic analysis of interviews and potential case study data will complement the quantitative findings. This phase explores the underlying mechanisms, contextual factors, and the 'why' behind observed statistical relationships, thereby strengthening causal inference through triangulation.

Step 1: Coding Qualitative Data

Transcripts and notes will be coded thematically, focusing on experiences related to network changes during crises, syndication behavior shifts, information flow, resource access, and perceived impacts on portfolio companies and overall ecosystem function, relevant to H1, H2, and H3.

Step 2: Identifying Mechanisms

Analysis will seek to understand the mechanisms behind quantitative findings. Why does fragmentation hinder recovery (H1)? How does bridging capital aid early-stage firms in crises (H2)? How does centralization support late-stage stability (H3)? Themes may include information access, coordination, trust, resource mobilization, and strategic adaptation differences based on network position and structure.

Step 3: Triangulation

Qualitative insights will be compared with quantitative results for H1, H2, and H3. Congruence strengthens interpretations (e.g., VCs describing how their bridging ties helped find scarce early-stage follow-on funding aligns with H2 quantitative results). Divergence prompts exploration of context or alternative explanations.

Conceptual Conclusion

Based on the illustrative findings for Hypotheses 1, 2, and 3, this research conceptually highlights potentially significant links between VC network dynamics and ecosystem resilience during economic downturns.

The mock results suggest that: (H1) network fragmentation may hinder recovery speed; (H2) pre-crisis bridging social capital might buffer early-stage funding from the worst downturn effects; and (H3) network centralization around experienced actors could support late-stage funding stability. Triangulation with qualitative data is crucial to understand the mechanisms driving these potential relationships (e.g., information flow, coordination, trust, resource access).

While conceptual, these potential findings underscore the importance of network structure in determining ecosystem resilience. Understanding these dynamics offers valuable insights for VCs, startups, LPs, and policymakers aiming to foster more robust innovation environments capable of weathering economic turbulence. Further empirical investigation is necessary to validate these relationships.
